### 6.3 Curriculum Improvement Areas

1. **Physics Stream (BES 121, BES 122, BES 282):** Three physics-related courses show failure rates of 5–39% and GPAs between 1.44–2.72. Content sequencing and prerequisite alignment should be reviewed. A bridging module or remedial physics track is recommended.

2. **Applied Mechanics Cluster (PRE 243, PRE 343, BES 141, BES 142):** Statics → Dynamics → Theory of Machines form a dependent chain; underperformance at earlier stages cascades forward. The program may benefit from modular prerequisites and embedded tutoring.

3. **Thermodynamics–Fluid Mechanics–Hydraulics Sequence (MPE 252, MPE 251, MPE 351):** All three related courses show failure rates of 12.5–26.9% with GPAs between 1.84–2.35. A coordinated instructional review of this sequence is warranted.

4. **Electronics Stream (ELE 211, ELE 231, ELE 413, ECE 323):** Consistent underperformance across electrical/electronic courses (GPA 1.64–2.55, failure 11–16%) points to gaps in student competency entering these courses, possibly from inadequate mathematics or physics preparation.

5. **Small-Cohort Courses (enrolled < 6):** Six courses have enrollments ≤ 5 students, making statistical inference unreliable (MEC 403, MEC 408, PRE 244, ECE 425, MEC 310, MEC 414). These should be excluded from performance benchmarking until cohort sizes normalize.

## SECTION 7 — RECOMMENDED ACTIONS

### 7.1 Immediate Actions (Next 4 Weeks)

- **[URGENT] Academic Support for Critical-Risk Courses:**
  Deploy structured tutoring programs or supplementary sessions for **BES 121 (Physics 1)**, **BES 282 (Modern Physics)**, and **MPE 252 (Thermodynamics)**. These three courses account for the majority of critical-risk registrations and represent a failure cohort of ~26 students.

- **[URGENT] Early-Warning System for Failing Students:**
  Identify students registered in two or more critical-risk courses simultaneously (likely Physics 1 + Statics or Physics 1 + Dynamics). Implement an early academic alert by Week 4 of the next semester — before withdrawal deadlines.

- **[HIGH] Instructor Data Completion:**
  Complete instructor field in the data system for the ≥20 courses currently lacking instructor attribution. This is prerequisite to faculty performance review and workload optimization.

### 7.2 Medium-Term Actions (Next Semester)

- **[HIGH] Curriculum Review — Physics & Applied Mechanics Sequences:**
  Convene a curriculum committee to review prerequisite chains in physics (BES 111 → BES 121 → BES 122 → BES 282) and mechanics (BES 141 → BES 142 → PRE 243 → PRE 343). Introduce formal prerequisite gates or co-requisites where gaps exist.

- **[HIGH] Teaching Method Improvement — Thermodynamics & Fluid Mechanics:**
  MPE 252 failure rate has doubled from the prior term. Evaluate teaching approach, exam design, and alignment with current regulation's learning outcomes. Consider problem-based learning or flipped classroom models for MPE 251 and MPE 252.

- **[MEDIUM] Faculty Monitoring for Worsening Courses:**
  Apply structured peer observation and student feedback review for courses with rapidly escalating failure rates: **ELE 413 (0% → 16.3%)**, **PRE 222 (3.7% → 18.8%)**, **MPE 252 (12.5% → 26.9%)**. Flag for mandatory annual teaching review.

- **[MEDIUM] Leverage High-Performing Course Models:**
  Conduct pedagogical case studies of MEC 201 (3.88 GPA, 2% failure), MEC 308 (3.89 GPA, 0% failure), and MEC 409 (3.31 GPA, 0% failure). Document and disseminate best practices to instructors of underperforming courses.

### 7.3 Long-Term Actions (Next Academic Year)

- **[MEDIUM] Student Cohort Tracking System:**
  Implement student-level tracking (not just course-level) to monitor GPA trajectories semester over semester. Current data only allows course-level analysis. A longitudinal student database would enable dropout risk prediction and retention interventions.

- **[MEDIUM] Standardize Data Collection:**
  Mandate instructor name completion, course section records, and section-level (not just course-level) grade distributions. This will raise the Data Quality Score from 82 to above 90.

- **[LOW] Review Small-Enrollment Course Viability:**
  Courses with ≤ 5 enrolled students (MEC 403, MEC 408, ECE 425, PRE 244) should be evaluated for consolidation, scheduling revision, or elimination to optimize resource allocation.

- **[LOW] Institutional Benchmarking Database:**
  Establish a historical GPA and failure rate baseline across three consecutive terms to enable statistically valid trend analysis. Currently, cross-term comparison is limited by curriculum regulation differences.

## SECTION 8 — ADDITIONAL STRATEGIC ANALYSIS & ACTION PLAN (APPENDED)

### 8.1 Root Cause Analysis (Additional Insight)

The current risk profile is best explained by systemic factors rather than isolated course incidents. The following root-cause clusters account for the majority of recurring academic underperformance:

1. **Foundation-to-Application Pipeline Fragility**
   - Core scientific and mathematical readiness is not consistently translating into success in downstream mechanics, thermodynamics, and electronics applications.
   - Students are entering application-heavy modules with uneven conceptual mastery, creating cumulative learning debt across the semester.

2. **Assessment-Load and Course-Demand Misalignment**
   - High-cognitive-load technical courses appear to rely on assessment structures that may over-emphasize high-stakes summative performance.
   - When assessment design is not tightly scaffolded to weekly learning progression, failure concentration increases in mid/late semester.

3. **Prerequisite Governance Gaps**
   - Existing prerequisite/co-requisite pathways function more as administrative sequencing than competency-based gating.
   - Students can progress despite unresolved foundational deficits, which amplifies risk in later integrated engineering courses.

4. **Instructional Design Variability Across Course Clusters**
   - Performance stability differs markedly between advanced/specialized offerings and several foundational technical streams, indicating uneven deployment of effective pedagogy.
   - The issue is likely less about curriculum intent and more about execution consistency (active learning, formative feedback frequency, tutorial support intensity).

5. **Decision-Support Data Maturity Constraints**
   - Course-level aggregates are useful for diagnosis but insufficient for real-time intervention targeting at student level.
   - Incomplete instructional metadata reduces the ability to link outcomes to teaching practices and implement evidence-based accountability loops.

### 8.2 High-Impact Priority Actions (Top Leverage Targets)

The following priority targets were selected based on cross-semester risk persistence, position in prerequisite chains, and volume-sensitive influence on program outcomes:

1. **Physics Foundation Stream (BES 121 + BES 282)**
   - **Why it matters:** This stream is an upstream dependency for multiple engineering pathways and is currently acting as an early academic bottleneck.
   - **Expected program-wide impact:** Strengthening this stream should reduce downstream failure propagation in mechanics/electronics and improve progression quality from early semesters onward.

2. **Mechanics Progression Chain (BES 141 / BES 142 / PRE 243 / PRE 343)**
   - **Why it matters:** This chain is structurally sequential; weak mastery in one stage compounds in subsequent courses.
   - **Expected program-wide impact:** Better alignment and mastery checkpoints here will lower cumulative risk and stabilize performance in design-oriented mechanical courses.

3. **Thermo-Fluid Engineering Block (MPE 252 + MPE 251)**
   - **Why it matters:** This block carries high conceptual density and contributes to repeated low-performance signals in applied engineering competency.
   - **Expected program-wide impact:** Intervention should improve technical competency attainment and decrease concentration of failures in core engineering science courses.

4. **Electronics Core Pathway (ELE 231 + ELE 413 + ECE 323)**
   - **Why it matters:** This pathway underpins mechatronics integration capability; instability here affects system-level engineering readiness.
   - **Expected program-wide impact:** Improved delivery and assessment coherence can raise capability in control, instrumentation, and embedded applications across the program.

5. **High-Enrollment Manufacturing/Production Gateway (PRE 151 + PRE 222)**
   - **Why it matters:** These courses influence a large segment of the cohort and represent high-leverage intervention points for throughput.
   - **Expected program-wide impact:** Gains here create immediate population-level improvement in retention momentum and semester GPA resilience.

- Implement early diagnostic baseline checks in the first three teaching weeks for prerequisite mastery.
- Introduce compulsory weekly formative checkpoints in flagged courses.
- Deploy coordinated tutoring/office-hour schedules tied to diagnostic outcomes, not voluntary attendance only.

- Apply prerequisite correction by introducing competency gates/co-requisites where sequencing currently lacks readiness assurance.
- Redesign assessment blueprints to balance formative/summative components and align exam cognitive level with taught learning outcomes.
- Standardize course-level intervention protocols for recurring risk categories.

**Phase 2 KPIs**

- Documented best-practice transfer plans executed for each targeted pathway.
- Measurable reduction in repeat-risk course count in annual quality review.
- Student mastery indicators show sustained improvement across sequential courses.

- Upgrade data architecture from course-aggregate monitoring to student-course longitudinal tracking.
- Deploy an early-warning engine combining attendance proxies, formative performance, and prior-term risk signals.
- Build a multi-term monitoring dashboard for progression, bottleneck detection, and intervention effectiveness.
- Enforce full instructional metadata completeness (instructor, section, intervention logs) for accountability analytics.

**Phase 4 KPIs**

- Longitudinal dashboard operational for multi-semester cohort tracking.
- Intervention outcome tracking incorporated into semester quality assurance cycle.
- Data governance compliance reaches accreditation-ready completeness standard.

### 8.4 Executive Insights (Additional Only)

- Program risk is concentrated in a small number of structurally connected pathways; targeted pathway repair will outperform diffuse, course-by-course remediation.
- The main strategic challenge is not absence of excellence but inconsistent transfer of effective teaching and assessment practice to foundational technical streams.
- Prerequisite policy should shift from sequencing control to demonstrated-competency control to prevent recurrent downstream underperformance.
- The highest-return governance move is integrating pedagogy, assessment, and progression analytics into one intervention framework rather than managing them in separate committees.
- The next quality ceiling will be determined by data maturity: student-level early warning and longitudinal evidence loops are now operational necessities, not optional enhancements.
